*Position your body towards the light of the screen.

This score performs cinema.

My personal history as a viewer can be enumerated not only as an inventory of moving images imprinted in my memory and practice, but also as a pattern of sitting and standing and their sensations. A habitual viewer, my body remembers postures, sanctioned fidgets, and contacts with chairs and benches and other bodily forms. I feel an incoherent and discontinuous timeline of calibration, of my body closening and distancing from screens amidst shifting qualities of ambient light. These feelings too are cinema, and its expansions. Removed from its architectures, I feel it more clearly now as a practice of positioning my body towards a light source, refracted and reflected.

Without the social contracts of the cinema and the gallery, I feel unsure how to watch: I don’t know what to do with my body. Feelings of failure -- I don’t know these moves -- permeate my virtual and remote viewing experiences.

Over these months I have considered what it would be like to re-choreography my viewing habits. I still don’t know, but I have tried different postures and patterns.

I recall that some of the earliest cinema-goers were unseated and looked down, not up. The kinetoscope invited an audience of one-at-a-time to stand and hunch over an eyepiece, their spines and gazes curving towards the peephole. The mechanisms of projecting moving images have always provided a physical score, directing bodies in their position to an image. In the arc of kinetoscope viewers’ backs, we can read cinema as a directive towards specific viewing postures. At its simplest, it is a proposition that we let our bodies be at rest, relatively still, in a dark, silent room.

And so, this I ask of you: position your body towards the light of the screen.

This is enough. You know how to do the rest.
